Key Concepts of Life Processes
•	Life Processes: The fundamental maintenance functions of living organisms that continuously prevent damage and structural breakdown, even during states of rest. 
•	Molecular Movement: The invisible, microscopic movement of molecules required to repair and maintain organized living structures (tissues and cells) against environmental decay. 
•	Nutrition: The process of transferring external energy sources (typically carbon-based food molecules) into the body to support maintenance, energy production, and growth. 
o	Autotrophic: Synthesizing complex organic compounds from simple inorganic raw materials ($CO_2$ and water) using sunlight and chlorophyll (Photosynthesis). 
o	Heterotrophic: Utilizing complex substances prepared by other organisms, broken down internally or externally via biological catalysts called enzymes. 
•	Respiration: The cellular process of breaking down food sources to generate a uniform energy currency (ATP). It includes aerobic respiration (utilizing oxygen in the mitochondria) and anaerobic respiration (occurring in the absence of oxygen). 
•	Transportation: A system of internal distribution required by complex multicellular organisms to carry food, gases, and metabolic wastes to and from cells that are not in direct contact with the external environment. 
•	Excretion: The biological mechanism responsible for filtering and removing toxic metabolic by-products (such as nitrogenous wastes) from the body. 
In-Depth Concept Relationships
The five primary life processes do not function in isolation; they form a tightly synchronized biochemical network where the output of one process serves as the vital baseline requirement for another.

1. Nutrition $\rightarrow$ Respiration (The Energy Cycle)
Nutrition provides the foundational raw materials (like glucose) needed for survival. However, raw food cannot directly power cellular maintenance. Respiration relies completely on nutrition to supply these carbon-based molecules, which it breaks down through oxidising-reducing reactions to produce ATP. Without nutrition, respiration lacks fuel; without respiration, nutrition cannot be converted into usable energy. 
2. Respiration $\leftrightarrow$ Transportation (The Gas & Power Delivery Network)
In single-celled organisms, simple diffusion manages gas exchange. In larger, complex multicellular organisms, diffusion is highly insufficient. 
•	Transportation sustains Respiration: The circulatory system uses a pumping organ (the heart) and respiratory pigments (haemoglobin) to carry oxygen from the respiratory surfaces (lungs/gills) directly to cells for aerobic respiration. 
•	Respiration sustains Transportation: Active transport mechanisms (such as the translocation of sucrose in plant phloem) rely directly on the chemical energy (ATP) generated by respiration to move materials against pressure gradients. 
3. Transportation $\rightarrow$ Excretion (The Waste Management Loop)
When cells utilize oxygen and carbon sources during respiration, they inevitably generate useless and highly toxic chemical by-products (like carbon dioxide, urea, or uric acid). If allowed to accumulate, these breakdown products would disrupt cellular order and cause death. The transportation system acts as a biological courier, pulling these metabolic wastes away from individual cells and delivering them directly to specialized excretory organs (the kidneys and nephrons) for selective filtration and ultimate disposal. 
4. Structural Design Convergence: Maximizing Surface Area
A striking relationship exists in how the body solves physical constraints across different systems through identical structural strategies. Nutrition, respiration, and excretion all optimize their performance by structurally maximizing internal surface area: 
•	In Nutrition: The small intestine develops finger-like projections called villi to increase surface area for absorbing digested nutrients into the bloodstream. 
•	In Respiration: The lungs branch into balloon-like structures called alveoli, providing a massive, delicate surface network optimized for rapid gas exchange. 
•	In Excretion: The kidneys house millions of highly coiled, thin-walled capillary clusters called nephrons to maximize the surface area available for filtering blood and reabsorbing essential water and salts.
